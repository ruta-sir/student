# CTE Pre-Expo Guest Speaker Reflection

---

Going into this talk, my expectations were narrow. I thought it would be a technical walkthrough, someone deep in computer science telling us how they built a voting platform, maybe some slides about architecture and code. What I got instead was something far different but just as crucial: a window into how a person actually becomes someone who builds things.
 
---
 
## The background behind product creation is crucial
 
The detail that stuck with me most wasn't about SafeBallot at all. It was everything *around* it, the basketball team he funded, the youth charity work, the highway project. These aren't resume bullet points. They're evidence of a person who sees a gap and moves toward it, whether or not it's in their job description.
 
That reframing matters to me. I've been guilty of thinking that the "real" work starts when I'm in a role and earned a position. But what he made clear is that character isn't something you turn on when the stakes feel high enough. You build it through the small, unrelated, seemingly off-topic choices you make every day. You fund the basketball team. You show up for the youth program. And when it's time to pitch, to hire, to lead, people already know who you are.
 
---
 
## Connections were monumental to his success
 
He talked a lot about connections — how important they were to getting SafeBallot off the ground. But the framing wasn't transactional. It wasn't *go meet people so they can help you later.* It was more fundamental than that: **you connect with people through genuine, unrelated experiences.** The relationships that end up mattering aren't the ones you engineered at a networking event. They're the ones that grew out of something real.
 
In CS, this hits differently. There's a tendency to think that the work speaks for itself — ship good code and the rest follows. But that's only ever half true. The people who helped him weren't just impressed by the product. They were invested in *him.* And that investment came from who he was outside the product.
 
I want to be more intentional about this going forward — not in a calculated way, but in a *show up fully* kind of way. Be present in the communities I'm part of. Don't save my personality for when it feels professionally relevant.
 
---
 
## Failure Is what taught him
 
He didn't build SafeBallot first. There were other businesses before it — attempts, pivots, things that didn't work. What he described wasn't a string of failures leading to eventual success. It was something subtler: each failure was *instructional.* Each "no" refined what he was building toward.
 
The line that will stay with me: **he didn't let one no throw him off but he let one yes push him forward.**
 
That's a different relationship with rejection than the one I've been trained to have. The instinct is to take rejection as information about whether you should keep going. What he modeled is treating it as information about *how* to keep going. Those are very different orientations.
 
This will shape how I approach projects, especially ones that stall or get shot down. A bad grade on a project, a rejected pull request, a feature that doesn't land the way I imagined, none of that is a verdict. It's data.
 
---
 
## You just need to know the next step, not the whole staircase
 
This was the most practically useful thing he said, and it applies directly to where I am right now as a CS student figuring out what I want to build and who I want to become.
 
**All you need is your start point, your end point, and your next step.**
 
I spend a lot of time waiting for clarity I'm not going to get in advance. What's my specialization? What kind of engineer do I want to be? What should I build? These feel like questions I need to answer before I can move, but that's not how it works. Movement is what generates the answers.
 
The staircase becomes visible as you climb it. That's not a lack of planning. That's just how building things actually goes.
 
---
 
## Think Like an Operator
 
One of the more unexpected ideas he introduced was the distinction between having a CEO title and having an operator's mentality. You don't need the former to practice the latter.
 
An operator mentality: doing something every day to get 1% better, building on consistency, taking ownership of your domain without waiting for permission is available to anyone at any level. As a student, as a junior engineer, as a collaborator on a team project, the mentality is already accessible. The question is whether I'm practicing it.
 
Football gave him that. The daily discipline, being held to a standard, wanting to give back. I want to find the equivalents in my own life and take them more seriously as genuine preparation for the work I want to do — not just extracurricular noise.
 
--- 

He also mentioned turning down investors, even when the offer was something he wanted. Not every yes deserves a yes back. Sometimes the terms, the fit, or the direction attached to that money isn't worth what it costs.
 
This is harder than it sounds. It requires knowing your own vision clearly enough to protect it when something shiny is being offered. That takes the kind of groundedness that only comes from knowing *why* you're building what you're building.
 
In a field where funding, job offers, and opportunities can feel scarce, this was a useful reminder that optionality isn't just about having choices — it's about being able to evaluate them clearly and say no when no is the right answer.
 
---
 
## What I'm Taking Forward
 
This talk pushed me to reframe a few things:
 
- **Consistency over intensity.** 1% better daily compounds. The dramatic sprint is less reliable than the daily practice.
- **Character is the product** — or at least, it shapes every product you build and every team you build it with.
- **Civic technology is underrated.** Building something that helps people participate in democracy — that's not a niche application. It's some of the highest-leverage work imaginable. I want to think bigger about what problems are worth solving with the skills I'm building.
- **Take the first step without the full map.** In CS and outside of it.
The biggest surprise of this talk was that none of the most valuable things I took from it were technical. The technical part — building SafeBallot, managing a team, navigating the product — was almost a backdrop to the real lesson, which was about the kind of person you have to become to make any of it possible.
